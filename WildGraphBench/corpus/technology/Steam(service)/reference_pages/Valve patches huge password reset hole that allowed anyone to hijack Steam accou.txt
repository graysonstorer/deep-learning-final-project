After losing control of their Steam accounts, some gamers and [Twitch streamers](https://www.reddit.com/r/Steam/comments/3elt4w/several_twitch_streamers_just_got_hijacked_and/) were definitely steamed. Valve blamed the account takeovers on a “[bug](http://kotaku.com/steam-accounts-hijacked-following-security-lapse-1720288836),” but the vulnerability seemed more like a critical hole – an authentication pit – since anyone could exploit it to hijack an account. Valve has patched the problem, but there is an [example](https://jsfiddle.net/valgaze/wpL2La7d/) of Steam’s password reset issue on JSFiddle for anyone who wants to try the exploit.

When logging into the Steam client, an attacker only needed to click on “forgot password” used to “retrieve” an account. That would take the attacker to a Steam Support page for “I forgot my Steam Account name or password,” according to a video showing the exploit. An attacker could enter an account name and “search.” After finding that account name, Steam would ask “How would you like to reset your password?”

An attacker would select “email an account recovery code to” 'whatever@whatever.com.' Steam Support would say it sent a recovery code to the email address and to enter the code received. However an attacker could leave the box for the account recovery code blank and instead hit “continue.” Steam Support would then take an attacker to the password reset page.

Valve has not made an [official statement](http://www.valvesoftware.com/news/) about the password reset vulnerability and that silence is nearly deafening since the security hole in Steam potentially affected millions of gamers. “Elm Hoe,” the guy explaining the Steam account hijack process in video, said Valve enacted a seven day ban on accounts accessed from a new device and a five day ban after a password change. Valve [told Kotaku](http://kotaku.com/steam-accounts-hijacked-following-security-lapse-1720288836) it knew about the “bug” on July 25 “that could have impacted the password reset process on a subset of Steam accounts during the period July 21-July 25. The bug has now been fixed.”

There are murmurings that having [Steam Guard](https://support.steampowered.com/kb_article.php?ref=4020-ALZM-5519&l=english) enabled did not protect accounts from being hijacked, although Valve claimed Steam Guard did protect accounts. Since several Twitch streamers had their Steam account hijacked, it makes you wonder…if a [person](https://twitter.com/paszaBiceps/status/625287812095590400)[games for living](https://twitter.com/olofmCS/status/625108278943596545) then surely he or she would have Steam Guard enabled? Gamers certainly should use Steam Guard.

Valve [told Kotaku](http://kotaku.com/steam-accounts-hijacked-following-security-lapse-1720288836):

> _To protect users, we are resetting passwords on accounts with suspicious password changes during that period or may have otherwise been affected. Relevant users will receive an email with a new password. Once that email is received, it is recommended that users login to their account via the Steam client and set a new password._
> 
> 
> _Please note that while an account password was potentially modified during this period the password itself was not revealed. Also, if Steam Guard was enabled, the account was protected from unauthorized logins even if the password was modified._
> 
> 
> _We apologize for any inconvenience._

It might be wise for gamers to use the [Steam Guard mobile authenticator](https://support.steampowered.com/kb_article.php?ref=8625-WRAH-9030), but the [Android version](https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.valvesoftware.android.steam.community) has 25,984 1-star ratings. Yet 236,655 Android users gave the app five stars and the[iOS version](https://itunes.apple.com/us/app/steam-mobile/id495369748?mt=8)is rated with three and half stars.

Steam Guard mobile authenticator screen capture from Steam "update news" on July 18.

The total Steam revenue in 2014 reached an estimated $1.5 billion.
