**National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA)**, independent U.S. governmental agency established in 1958 for the [research and development](https://www.britannica.com/topic/research-and-development) of vehicles and activities for the [exploration of space](https://www.britannica.com/science/space-exploration) within and outside [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth)â€™s [atmosphere](https://www.britannica.com/science/atmosphere).

[![Image 20: Johnson Space Center: control room](https://cdn.britannica.com/02/112702-050-7338D3A3/Control-room-Johnson-Space-Center-Texas-Houston.jpg?w=300)](https://cdn.britannica.com/02/112702-050-7338D3A3/Control-room-Johnson-Space-Center-Texas-Houston.jpg)

[Johnson Space Center: control room](https://cdn.britannica.com/02/112702-050-7338D3A3/Control-room-Johnson-Space-Center-Texas-Houston.jpg)Control room of the Johnson Space Center, Houston, Texas.(more)

The organization is composed of four mission directorates: Aeronautics Research, for the development of advanced [aviation](https://www.britannica.com/technology/aviation) technologies; Science, dealing with programs for understanding the origin, structure, and evolution of the [universe](https://www.britannica.com/science/universe), the [solar system](https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-system), and Earth; Space Technology, for the development of space science and exploration technologies; and Human Exploration and Operations, concerning the management of crewed space missions, including those to the [International Space Station](https://www.britannica.com/topic/International-Space-Station), as well as operations related to launch services, space transportation, and space communications for both crewed and robotic exploration programs. A number of additional research centres are [affiliated](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/affiliated), including the [Goddard Space Flight Center](https://www.britannica.com/place/Goddard-Space-Flight-Center) in Greenbelt, Maryland; the [Jet Propulsion Laboratory](https://www.britannica.com/place/Jet-Propulsion-Laboratory) in Pasadena, California; the [Johnson Space Center](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Lyndon-B-Johnson-Space-Center) in [Houston](https://www.britannica.com/place/Houston), Texas; and the Langley Research Center in Hampton, Virginia. Headquarters of NASA are in Washington, D.C.

NASA was created largely in response to the Soviet launching of [Sputnik](https://www.britannica.com/technology/Sputnik) in 1957. It was organized around the [National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics](https://www.britannica.com/topic/National-Advisory-Committee-for-Aeronautics-United-States) (NACA), which had been created by Congress in 1915. NASAâ€™s organization was well under way by the early years of Pres. [John F. Kennedy](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-F-Kennedy)â€™s administration when he proposed that the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) put a man on the [Moon](https://www.britannica.com/place/Moon) by the end of the 1960s. To that end, the [Apollo program](https://www.britannica.com/science/Apollo-space-program) was designed, and in 1969 the U.S. astronaut [Neil Armstrong](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Neil-Armstrong) became the first person on the Moon. All told, during nine Apollo missions, 24 astronauts (all Americans) [went to the Moon](https://www.britannica.com/story/how-many-people-have-been-to-the-moon), and 12 of them walked on it. Later, uncrewed programsâ€”such as [Viking](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Viking-space-probe), [Mariner](https://www.britannica.com/technology/Mariner), [Voyager](https://www.britannica.com/technology/Voyager-space-probes), and [Galileo](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Galileo-spacecraft)â€”explored other bodies of the solar system.

NASA was also responsible for the development and launching of a number of satellites with Earth applications, such as [Landsat](https://www.britannica.com/technology/Landsat), a series of satellites designed to collect information on natural resources and other Earth features; [communications satellites](https://www.britannica.com/technology/communications-satellite); and weather satellites. It also planned and developed the [space shuttle](https://www.britannica.com/technology/space-shuttle), a reusable vehicle capable of carrying out missions that could not be conducted with [conventional](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/conventional)[spacecraft](https://www.britannica.com/technology/spacecraft).

As part of the[Artemis](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Artemis-program)space [program](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Artemis-program), launched in 2017,NASA aims to return humans to the Moon by 2025, with the goal of establishing a sustainable presence there and on other planets. The program also seeks to land the first woman and first person of colour on the Moon, and that woman may be[Jessica Meir](https://www.britannica.com/story/7-questions-for-astronaut-jessica-meir).
