Commercial Space
================

NASA is enabling commercial industry to build, own, and operate space systems with the agency purchasing services for its science and research needs. Through public-private partnerships, NASA is opening space to more science, people, and opportunities.

![Image 37: Earth at night, including the Earth's curvature, city lights, oceans, clouds, and the atmosphere](https://www.nasa.gov/wp-content/uploads/2022/12/iss041e012095orig.jpg?w=2048)

Low Earth Orbit Economy
-----------------------

As the International Space Station nears the end of operations, NASA plans to transition to a new low Earth orbit model to continue leveraging microgravity benefits. Through commercial partnerships, NASA aims to maintain its leadership in microgravity research and ensure continued benefits for humanity.

[Learn More about Low Earth Orbit Economy](https://www.nasa.gov/humans-in-space/commercial-space/low-earth-orbit-economy/)

![Image 38: The moon appears in front of the International Space Station's truss structure](https://www.nasa.gov/wp-content/uploads/2022/12/53234719489-bfa88b3c5d-o.jpg?w=2048)

Private Astronaut Missions
--------------------------

Private astronaut missions to the International Space Station help pave the way toward commercial space stations as part of NASA’s efforts to develop a thriving low Earth orbit ecosystem and marketplace.

[Learn More about Private Astronaut Missions](https://www.nasa.gov/humans-in-space/private-astronaut-missions/)

![Image 39: Four astronauts wearing black Axiom Space polo shirts standing with their arms crossed](https://www.nasa.gov/wp-content/uploads/2022/12/ax4-axiom-announcementportraits-prime-20240904-0002.jpg?w=1536)

The Axiom Mission 4, or Ax-4, crew will launch aboard a SpaceX Dragon spacecraft to the International Space Station from NASA’s Kennedy Space Center in Florida. From left to right: ISRO (Indian Space Research Organization) astronaut Shubhanshu Shukla, former NASA astronaut Peggy Whitson, ESA (European Space Agency) astronaut Sławosz Uznański-Wiśniewski of Poland, and Tibor Kapu of Hungary.

Credit: Axiom Space

Commercial Space Stations
-------------------------

NASA is committed to maintaining a continuous human presence in low Earth orbit as the agency transitions from the International Space Station to commercial space stations.

[Learn More about Commercial Space Stations](https://www.nasa.gov/humans-in-space/commercial-space/low-earth-orbit-economy/commercial-destinations-in-low-earth-orbit/)

Growing the Lunar Economy
-------------------------

NASA is leading Artemis, humanity’s return to the Moon. With international partners and U.S. industry providers, the future at the Moon holds promise for a robust lunar marketplace.

[Learn More about Growing the Lunar Economy](https://www.nasa.gov/humans-in-space/growing-the-lunar-economy/)

![Image 41: Illustration of the lunar surface, featuring astronauts, robotic machinery, a partially assembled base, and the Earth visible in the background](https://www.nasa.gov/wp-content/uploads/2022/12/1-icon-olympus-multi-purpose-isru-based-lunarconstructionsystem-concept-render-nov2022-for-icon-release.jpeg?w=2048)

Human Landing Systems
---------------------

NASA’s commercial providers, SpaceX and Blue Origin, are building the human landing systems that will carry Artemis astronauts to the lunar surface and back to lunar orbit for their ride home to Earth aboard the Orion spacecraft.

[Learn More about Human Landing Systems](https://www.nasa.gov/reference/human-landing-systems/)

![Image 42: Side-by-side illustrations of the SpaceX Starship lunar lander and the Blue Origin Blue Moon lunar lander. Each is on the lunar surface, with astronauts nearby and Earth in the distance.](https://www.nasa.gov/wp-content/uploads/2023/06/starship-bluemoon.png?w=1536)

Side-by-side illustrations of the SpaceX Starship lunar lander and the Blue Origin Blue Moon lunar lander. 

Credit: SpaceX/Blue Origin

Commercial Lunar Payload Services
---------------------------------

NASA is working with several American companies to deliver science and technology to the lunar surface through the Commercial Lunar Payload Services initiative. These companies, ranging in size, bid on delivering payloads for NASA. Under Artemis, commercial deliveries are performing science experiments, testing technologies, and demonstrating capabilities to help NASA explore the Moon.

[Learn More about Commercial Lunar Payload Services](https://www.nasa.gov/commercial-lunar-payload-services/)

![Image 43: Image of the moon's surface taken by Lunar Orbiter 3](https://www.nasa.gov/wp-content/uploads/2023/04/lunar-orbiter-3-image-catalog.jpg?w=1288)

The moon’s surface taken by Lunar Orbiter 3.

Credit: NASA

Commercial Crew Program
-----------------------

NASA’s Commercial Crew Program is delivering on its goal of safe, reliable, and cost-effective human transportation to and from the International Space Station through a partnership with American private industry.

[Learn More about Commercial Crew Program](https://www.nasa.gov/humans-in-space/commercial-space/commercial-crew-program/)

![Image 44: The four astronauts of SpaceX Crew-6 wearing white spacesuits with helmets stand in front of a rocket gantry at night](https://www.nasa.gov/wp-content/uploads/2022/12/53556655334-0f9f126935-o.jpg?w=2048)

Launch Services Program
-----------------------

NASA’s Launch Service Program is responsible for launching commercial rockets delivering spacecraft that observe the Earth, visit other planets, and explore the universe, ranging from weather satellites and telescopes to Mars Rovers.

[Learn More about Launch Services Program](https://www.nasa.gov/kennedy/launch-services-program/)

![Image 45: Two rockets launch, emitting bright white light into a dark blue dusk sky, with the right rocket arcing out and away, disappearing into the sky](https://www.nasa.gov/wp-content/uploads/2022/12/vsfb-20250311-ph-spx01-0002orig.jpg?w=1536)

A SpaceX Falcon 9 rocket, carrying NASA’s SPHEREx (Spectro-Photometer for the History of the Universe, Epoch of Reionization and Ices Explorer) observatory and PUNCH (Polarimeter to Unify the Corona and Heliosphere) satellites, launches from Space Launch Complex 4 East at Vandenberg Space Force Base in California.
