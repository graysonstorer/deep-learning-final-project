Coronavirus Research Is Moving at Top Speed‚ÄîWith a Catch
========================================================

Scientists are posting their papers on the China outbreak as fast as they can write them, skipping traditional journals.

![Image 4: 2 medical workers using syringes and pipettes](https://media.wired.com/photos/5e3327d156ba350008f0cff1/3:2/w_2560%2Cc_limit/Science_preprintscoronavirus_RTS2ZZDE.jpg)

Photograph: Reuters

Jonathan Read admits to being something of a dinosaur [when it comes to publishing](https://www.wired.com/tag/scientific-publishing/) his work. An epidemiologist at Lancaster University in the UK, Read had always followed the old ways‚Äîsubmit to a journal, get accepted, get comments and edits from peer reviewers, revise the article, publish.

But a few years ago, something started nagging at him. That process typically moves a lot slower than a disease outbreak. And even when it moves fast, it can involve considerations besides rigor. Submitting papers on Ebola during the summer of 2014, Read says he felt like his team was getting overlooked by journals in favor of research that‚Äôd garner more attention from journalists upon publication. ‚ÄúI remember thinking at the time, ‚ÄòNext time this happens, we should just blog it,‚Äô‚Äù Read says.

Traditional journals have activated various emergency protocols to speed things up during the outbreak‚Äîfaster publication cycles and dropped paywalls, primarily. Teams of scientists around the world are bypassing them anyway, communicating not only their early-days conclusions but also their methods and approaches. Researchers say this time the speed and sheer number of preprints has been unprecedented.

That‚Äôs good! Because it makes science faster and better, and it‚Äôs helping life scientists join other fields in embracing a new mode. But it‚Äôs also a little scary. Scientists aren‚Äôt the only ones who can download a preprint. That opens up the work to possible misunderstanding or misinterpretation.

This week, coronavirus papers dominated the top 10 spots on Rxivist, which tracks traffic and topics on bioRxiv. When I spoke to Ran Blekhman, the University of Minnesota genomics researcher who runs Rxivist as a side gig, people had downloaded the top article 29,000 times. ‚ÄúThis paper was published six days ago, and it‚Äôs already the most downloaded paper of all time in the microbiology category and the 17th-most downloaded paper overall,‚Äù Blekhman says. ‚ÄúI haven‚Äôt seen that before.‚Äù

Some of that new interest is because preprints help move the science forward. ‚ÄúThe primary benefit is probably in scientists being able to improve their work, to see what other scientists are working on, and come up with some consensus,‚Äù says Maia Majumder, a computational epidemiologist at Boston Children‚Äôs Hospital and Harvard Medical School. ‚ÄúFor outbreaks especially, I think no matter how hard a journal tries to make review as rapid as possible, there‚Äôs still going to be a delay.‚Äù Not so for preprint servers.

It helps, too, that biomedical journals have gotten a lot clearer about their willingness to publish, after traditional review, articles that have already been out in the world via preprint servers. That wasn‚Äôt always the case. But now heavy-hitter journals like _The Lancet_, _Science_, and _The New England Journal of Medicine_ have all told researchers that a preprint doesn‚Äôt take an article out of the running‚Äîalbeit with [varying degrees of clarity](https://www.sciencemag.org/news/2019/06/medical-preprint-server-debuts) and completeness. That policy shift removed some of the disincentive; now nobody has to balance the ethics of publishing potentially life-saving work in a fast-moving field against the necessities of getting published in journals to get grants and tenure. In fact, about [two-thirds of articles](https://elifesciences.org/articles/45133) posted on the preprint server bioRxiv go on to be published in peer-reviewed journals.

That said, the old-school journals still attempt to distinguish between the two flavors of article. ‚ÄúWe don‚Äôt want to publish something that‚Äôs substandard and doesn‚Äôt merit the attention of our audience. Physicians expect us to be selective and help them focus,‚Äù says Edward Campion, executive editor of _NEJM_, which Campion says has gotten more than 50 submissions on coronavirus in the five days before we spoke. ‚ÄúWhat we‚Äôre publishing is peer reviewed and vetted and selected.‚Äù

_NEJM_ has, like other big journals, expedited its own publication procedures, as it has during other public health crises. ‚ÄúWe have a lot of resources, and we‚Äôre able to handle things very quickly,‚Äù Campion says. ‚ÄúSome of these things have been publishing in less than 48 hours, even 24 hours, still maintaining our standards.‚Äù

Preprint adherents argue that in their case the review process happens‚Äîbut after publication instead of before. And they can make sure their work stays up to date in a fast-moving information environment.

The flip side of that interest, though, is that new, rawer scientific work filters out to the general public much more quickly, without interpretation or intervention. Sometimes that can mean way-out theories get taken seriously, like the idea that the new coronavirus is some kind of bioweapon. (There‚Äôs no evidence that it is.) But even mainstream ideas need honing. ‚ÄúIf it hasn‚Äôt been peer-reviewed yet, you have to be super-explicit about that if you‚Äôre planning to share that work with a broader audience,‚Äù Majumder says. ‚ÄúPeople will post something on a preprint server, pop it onto Twitter, and then disappear for 24 hours. And then it ends up in sheer chaos, which has happened a few times already with this particular outbreak.‚Äù

That‚Äôs what happened with Read‚Äôs team. They calculated a terrifyingly high R0, the average number of people an infected human will give the virus in turn.
