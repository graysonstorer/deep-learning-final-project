### [![Image 6: (up)](http://primes.utm.edu/top20/includes/gifs/up3.gif)](http://primes.utm.edu/top20/page.php?id=1#top) Definitions and Notes

Twin [primes](http://primes.utm.edu/glossary/xpage/Prime.html) are pairs of primes which differ by two. The first [twin primes](http://primes.utm.edu/glossary/xpage/TwinPrime.html) are {3,5}, {5,7}, {11,13} and {17,19}. It has been [conjectured](http://primes.utm.edu/glossary/xpage/Conjecture.html) (but never proven) that there are infinitely many twin primes. _If_ the probability of a random [integer](http://primes.utm.edu/glossary/xpage/Integer.html)_n_ and the integer _n_+2 being prime were statistically independent events, then it would follow from the [prime number theorem](http://primes.utm.edu/glossary/xpage/PrimeNumberThm.html) that there are about _n_/([log](http://primes.utm.edu/glossary/xpage/Log.html)_n_)2 twin primes less than or equal to _n_. These probabilities are not independent, so Hardy and Littlewood conjectured that the correct estimate should be the following. 
> ![Image 7: It's a black image](http://primes.utm.edu/gifs/HardyLittlewood2.gif)

 Here the [infinite](http://primes.utm.edu/glossary/xpage/Infinite.html) product is the [twin prime constant](http://primes.utm.edu/glossary/xpage/TwinPrimeConstant.html) (estimated by Wrench and others to be approximately 0.6601618158...), and we introduce an integral to improve the quality of the estimate. This estimate works quite well! For example: 
The number of twin primes

less than _N_| _N_ | actual | estimate |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 10 6 | 8169 | 8248 |
| 10 8 | 440312 | 440368 |
| 10 10 | 27412679 | 27411417 |

There is a longer table by Kutnib and Richstein available online.

In 1919 Brun showed that the sum of the reciprocals of the twin primes converges to a sum now called Brun's Constant. (Recall that the [sum of the reciprocals of all primes diverges](http://primes.utm.edu/infinity.shtml#punchline).) By calculating the twin primes up to 10 14 (and discovering the [infamous pentium bug](http://www.trnicely.net/#PENT) along the way), [Thomas Nicely](http://www.trnicely.net/index.html)_[heuristically](http://primes.utm.edu/glossary/xpage/Heuristic.html)_ estimates [Brun's constant](http://primes.utm.edu/glossary/xpage/BrunsConstant.html) to be 1.902160578.

As an exercise you might want to prove the following version of [Wilson's theorem](http://primes.utm.edu/glossary/xpage/WilsonsTheorem.html).

**Theorem:** (Clement 1949) The integers _n_, _n_+2, form a pair of twin primes if and only if 
> 4[(_n_-1)!+1] ![Image 8: =](http://primes.utm.edu/gifs/congruent.gif)-_n_([mod](http://primes.utm.edu/glossary/xpage/Congruence.html)_n_(_n_+2)).

 Nice--too bad it is of virtually no practical value!
