### [![Image 4: (up)](http://primes.utm.edu/top20/includes/gifs/up3.gif)](http://primes.utm.edu/top20/page.php?id=30#top) Definitions and Notes

Factorial [primes](http://primes.utm.edu/glossary/xpage/Prime.html) come in two flavors: [factorial plus one](http://primes.utm.edu/glossary/xpage/FactorialPrime.html): _n_!+1, and [factorial minus one](http://primes.utm.edu/glossary/xpage/FactorialPrime.html): _n_!-1. The form _n_!+1 is prime for _n_=1, 2, 3, 11, 27, 37, 41, 73, 77, 116, 154, 320, 340, 399, 427, 872, 1477 and 6380 (21507 digits). (See [Borning72], [Templer80], [BCP82], and [Caldwell95].) The form _n_!-1 is prime for _n_=3, 4, 6, 7, 12, 14, 30, 32, 33, 38, 94, 166, 324, 379, 469, 546, 974, 1963, 3507, 3610 and 6917 (23560 digits). Both forms have been tested to _n_=10000 [CG2000]. 
There is more information of [primorial](http://primes.utm.edu/glossary/xpage/Primorial.html) and [factorial primes](http://primes.utm.edu/glossary/xpage/FactorialPrime.html) in [Dubner87] and [Dubner89a].
