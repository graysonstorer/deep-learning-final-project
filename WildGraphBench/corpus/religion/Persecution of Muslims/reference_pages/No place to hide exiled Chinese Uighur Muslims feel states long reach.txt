_**by Jo√´lle Garrus**_

Sitting on the terrace of a Paris cafe, a young Chinese woman glances nervously at her mobile phone as a message from a police officer in her native Xinjiang region pops up.

A member of China‚Äôs Muslim Uighur minority, Mariem travelled halfway around the world to study in France, but has found herself pulled into a mass security crackdown under way back home.

[![Image 22: HKFP Support Nov 2025](https://hongkongfp.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/06/HKFP-Support-Nov-2025.png)](https://hongkongfp.com/support-hong-kong-free-press/)

![Image 24: Uighur china turkestan](https://hongkongfp.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/08/000_1768EQ.jpg)

A demonstrator wearing a mask painted with the colours of the flag of East Turkestan and a hand bearing the colours of the Chinese flag. File photo: Ozan Kose/AFP.

‚ÄúThey want to know where I live, what I do, how I spend the weekend. They want me to give them information about Uighurs here. They threaten my family who beg me do do what they ask,‚Äù she said, visibly stressed.

Mariem is one of several ethnic Uighurs in France who shared accounts of harassment by Chinese authorities and expressed concern for missing family members caught up in sweeping security operations back home.

All their names have been changed to protect their identity.

AFP saw messages several Uighurs received from the authorities on China‚Äôs messaging application WeChat.

‚ÄúDo you have your degree now?‚Äù one officer asks a student.

‚ÄúSend me your address and tell me who you work for and what your degree is,‚Äù reads another message.

‚ÄúWhy don‚Äôt you send photos?‚Äù another person returning from holidays is asked.

**‚ÄòOrwellian society‚Äô**

On Monday, Chinese officials were grilled by a UN human rights committee in Geneva over reports that it is holding up to one million Muslims, mostly Uighurs, in camps under cover of a massive anti-terrorism drive.

China has pointed to a series of attacks in Xinjiang by suspected Islamist radicals in recent years as justification for a draconian clampdown in a region with a long history of tensions with Beijing.

But it has called the reports of internment camps ‚Äúcompletely untrue‚Äù, saying that the ‚Äúeducation and training centres‚Äù to which ‚Äúminor criminals‚Äù are assigned serve merely ‚Äúto assist in their rehabilitation and reintegration‚Äù.

![Image 26: YouTube video](https://i.ytimg.com/vi/NO0dRYffeng/hqdefault.jpg)

Several NGOs and China experts believe that what is under way is far more sinister, saying accounts from former detainees and official documents point to a massive programme of political and cultural indoctrination.

Last year,China banned ‚Äúabnormally long‚Äù beards and Muslim veils in Xinjiang and ordered all car owners in the region to install GPS tracking devices.

In December, New York-based Human Rights Watch reported that Xinjiang authorities were planning to collect biodata from all residents.

‚ÄúAn Orwellian society has been put in place in Xinjiang,‚Äù Thierry Kellner, a politics professor at Belgium‚Äôs Universite Libre de Bruxelles specialising in China and the Muslim world, told AFP.

A report published by US-based security analysis group Jamestown Foundation in May estimated that ‚Äúat least several hundred thousand and possibly just over one million‚Äù people had been interned as part of a ‚Äúpacification drive‚Äù.

‚ÄúThis is unprecedented and exceeds anything that China has done in any other region including Tibet,‚Äù the report‚Äôs author, Adrian Zenz, told AFP.

Surveillance of overseas Uighurs is also ‚Äúa very common practice‚Äù, Kellner said.

![Image 28: Kashgar Xinjiang](https://hongkongfp.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/08/chrome_2018-08-07_14-38-43.jpg)

Kashgar, Xinjiang in 2017. File photo: David Stanley, via Flickr.

Questioned about the allegations the Chinese foreign ministry said it was ‚Äúnot aware‚Äù of any such practices.

**Passport ‚Äòblackmail‚Äô**

Nijat, a young man who arrived in France on a student visa in 2007, deleted his WeChat account after getting a call from a person claiming to be a police officer who asked for a copy of his passport and visa.

‚ÄúHe said if I didn‚Äôt cooperate my family would get into trouble,‚Äù he told AFP.

For months now he has been without news of his sister and his parents, who asked his brother in Canada to stop calling home, fearing the contact was inviting unwelcome attention.

![Image 30: XinJiang, China](https://hongkongfp.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/11/2000px-Xinjiang_in_China_de-facto.svg_.jpg)

XinJiang, China. Photo: Wikicommons.

At a Uighur rally in Paris in July, AFP also met Adil, who held aloft a photograph of his grandmother and brother, who he claimed were being held in camps in Xinjiang.

Adil first moved to Turkey, which has close religious and cultural ties to Xinjiang.

But after Turkey began cracking down on ‚Äúanti-Chinese‚Äù forces at Beijing‚Äôs request he fled to France, leaving behind another brother, of whom he also has no news.

Mariem says her family is also in the state‚Äôs sights. One of her brothers has been arrested and the other has disappeared.

![Image 32: Xinjiang police in Urumqi](https://hongkongfp.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/Armed_Police_armored_vehicles_in_Urumqi2.jpg)

Armed police in √úr√ºmqi, Xinjiang. Photo: Wikicommons.

‚ÄúI cannot get hold of him. When I ask about him I get conflicting information. I think he has been sent to a camp,‚Äù she said.

She fears she could face a similar fate if forced to return home to renew her passport, a service usually available to overseas citizens through their embassy but which has been denied to her.

Questioned by AFP about similar claims of passport ‚Äúblackmail‚Äù from other France-based Uighurs, the Chinese foreign ministry said that the issuing of travel documents was China‚Äôs ‚Äúinternal affairs‚Äù.

‚ÄúChinese embassies and consulates have always been issuing passports and other travel documents to overseas Chinese citizens in accordance with laws and regulations,‚Äù it added.
